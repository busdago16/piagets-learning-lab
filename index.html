The theorist

Why Piaget still matters

1896–1980
JP

Jean Piaget

Piaget studied how children make sense of the world. His constructivist perspective focuses on the individual learner and the active processes through which knowledge is built and reorganized.

Learning is not simply receiving information. Learners interpret new experiences through what they already know—and sometimes must reorganize that knowledge.

Adapted from the constructivist principles discussed by Kalina and Powell (2009).

MENTAL FRAMEWORK

Schemas

Organized patterns of thought or action that learners use to understand and respond to experiences.

Example: A child may have a schema that “four-legged furry animals are dogs.”
PHYSICAL

Knowledge of objects

Built through direct interaction with the properties of objects and the physical world.

LOGICO-MATHEMATICAL

Knowledge of relationships

Constructed by mentally organizing patterns, quantities, classifications, and relationships.

SOCIAL

Knowledge from people

Conventions, language, values, and shared meanings learned through social interaction.

Theory into design

Piaget in online learning

Online environments become constructivist when learners do more than watch, click “next,” and memorize.

01

Active discovery

Give learners opportunities to investigate, manipulate, make predictions, and test possible explanations.

  • Interactive simulations
  • Virtual labs
  • Inquiry activities
  • Problem-solving games
02

Developmentally appropriate design

Match language, abstraction, feedback, controls, and task complexity to learners' developmental readiness.

  • Concrete visual supports
  • Age-appropriate instructions
  • Scaffolded complexity
  • Meaningful interaction
03

Productive cognitive conflict

Use surprising outcomes and challenging problems to expose misconceptions and encourage schema revision.

  • Unexpected simulation results
  • Branching scenarios
  • Prediction and explanation
  • Assumption-testing puzzles

A critical lens

Strengths and limitations

Useful theories become more powerful when we understand where they help—and where they oversimplify.

+

What Piaget offers online learning

  • A strong argument for active rather than passive learning.
  • A focus on prior knowledge and schema development.
  • A reason to use exploration, prediction, feedback, and revision.
  • A reminder to consider developmental readiness when designing tasks.
  • Support for practical, verbal, abstract, and creative reasoning when activities are appropriately designed.
!

Where caution is needed

  • Children do not always progress through rigid, universal stage boundaries.
  • Performance can vary by task, context, prior experience, culture, and support.
  • Some abilities may appear earlier than Piaget proposed.
  • Individual constructivism can understate the importance of social interaction.
  • Digital tools cannot fully replace embodied, physical, and human experiences.
